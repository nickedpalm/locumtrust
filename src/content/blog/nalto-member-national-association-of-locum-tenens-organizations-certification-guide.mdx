A hospital administrator I spoke with once told me she'd hired a locum tenens agency she found through a Google ad. The agency's website looked professional. The doctor showed up. But the credentialing file was a mess — board certification listed without dates, malpractice coverage that turned out to be lapsed. She spent three weeks cleaning it up while the physician was already on shift.

"I didn't know to ask if they were NALTO members," she said. "I didn't even know what NALTO was."

Neither did I, the first time I started researching this space. Now I do. And the honest answer is: it matters, but not as much as the trade associations want you to believe — and less than you'd think if you just read agency websites.

> **The Short Version:** NALTO membership signals that an agency has committed to a formal code of ethics and industry credentialing standards. It's a meaningful filter — but it's not a quality guarantee. Non-members can be excellent. Members can be mediocre. It's a starting condition, not a finishing one.

## Key Takeaways

- NALTO (National Association of Locum Tenens Organizations) has grown to **160+ member agencies** over two decades, making it the dominant industry credentialing body.
- Members commit to a written Code of Ethics covering honesty, integrity, and specific credentialing verification requirements — including education, board certification, and work history.
- Membership doesn't guarantee performance, response time, specialty depth, or competitive rates. Those vary wildly inside the member pool.
- For facilities hiring locum physicians, NALTO membership is a useful initial filter — not a replacement for your own due diligence.

## What NALTO Actually Is

NALTO — the National Association of Locum Tenens Organizations — is a trade association for staffing firms that place temporary physicians and advanced practice providers. It was founded during a genuine crisis: early locum tenens companies were operating without consistent standards, malpractice coverage was a mess, and the industry's reputation was suffering for it.

Mike DeCamp, an industry veteran, put it plainly: *"We sold it hard to clients and candidates that if you were going to use a locum tenens company, you better make sure they're a NALTO member."*

That origin story matters. NALTO didn't emerge from a marketing meeting — it emerged from a malpractice crisis. The founding goal was legitimacy, not brand differentiation.

Today, NALTO's value proposition rests on three pillars: a **Code of Ethics** that all members must follow, **credentialing best practices** formalized in published guidelines, and **industry education** through annual conventions (co-hosted with NAPR, the National Association of Physician Recruiters) and continuing education webinars.

It's a real institution. It's done real work. It's not a rubber stamp.

## The Credentialing Standard (This Is the Part That Actually Matters)

Here's what most people miss when they see "NALTO member" on an agency website: the credentialing requirements are specific and meaningful.

NALTO's 2024 *Best Practices in Credentialing* guidelines require member agencies to verify — not just collect — the following:

| Credential Element | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Full legal name, DOB, SSN | Collected for identity verification |
| Medical education & training | Month/year dates required (not just institution names) |
| Board certification status | Verification against primary source |
| Work history | Gaps must be accounted for |
| Malpractice coverage | Resources provided; current status required |

The word "verify" is doing a lot of work there. Plenty of non-NALTO agencies collect documents. The standard is whether someone actually calls the medical board, confirms the dates, and flags the gaps. NALTO members are committed to that standard in writing.

Does every NALTO agency execute it perfectly every time? No. But they've signed on to be held to it. That's not nothing.

> **Reality Check:** NALTO has no enforcement mechanism that functions like a medical licensing board. If a member agency cuts corners on credentialing and nothing blows up publicly, there's limited consequence. Membership raises the floor — it doesn't eliminate bad actors.

## When NALTO Membership Actually Matters

**You're a hospital administrator filling an unexpected gap.** A NALTO-member agency means a physician candidate has been run through a documented credentialing process. You're not starting from zero on verification. Time is scarce; baseline standards help.

**You're a physician evaluating agencies for the first time.** NALTO membership is a signal that an agency has been around long enough to join, understands industry norms, and has resources (including malpractice insurance access) built into their infrastructure. It's a faster way to build a shortlist.

**You're in a compliance-sensitive environment.** Government and commercial facilities operating under managed care contracts need to demonstrate that coverage providers meet professional standards. A NALTO-member agency adds a layer of documentation to that story.

> **Pro Tip:** NALTO co-hosts its annual convention with NAPR. The agencies that show up, present, and participate in industry education tend to be the serious operators. Attendance isn't public, but it's worth asking a prospective agency whether they send staff to the annual convention — and whether they've presented or led sessions there.

## When It Doesn't Matter (Or Matters Less Than You'd Think)

**You've already vetted the agency through direct experience.** Referrals from trusted colleagues beat any certification. A smaller non-NALTO agency that's placed twenty physicians at a facility similar to yours, without a single credentialing complaint, is a stronger signal than a large NALTO member you know nothing about.

**You're comparing agencies within the member pool.** NALTO has 160+ members. That's a wide range — from boutique specialty shops to national firms with hundreds of placements per year. Membership status doesn't differentiate inside that pool. Specialty depth, fill rates, response times, and contract terms do.

**The physician already has verified credentials on file.** If a facility is re-engaging a physician they've worked with before through any agency, the NALTO status of the new agency matters less than the existing credential file.

Nobody tells you this, but the NALTO badge on an agency's homepage is mostly marketing at the moment of first impression. The actual value is structural — it means the agency built their processes around written industry standards from the beginning.

## The NALTO Life Member Wrinkle

One detail worth knowing: NALTO offers life membership to individuals after **10 years** in good standing who've retired from regular employment. Life members retain access to all NALTO benefits except board and officer positions.

This is relevant when evaluating agency leadership. If a firm's founder or senior leadership holds NALTO life membership, it's a reasonable signal of sustained industry engagement — not just dues paid this year.

---

## Practical Bottom Line

Use NALTO membership as a **filter, not a verdict**.

When building a shortlist of locum tenens agencies — whether you're a facility administrator or a physician — start with NALTO members to establish baseline credentialing standards. Then evaluate from there on the things NALTO can't measure: specialty coverage depth, how fast they fill an assignment, what their guarantee policy looks like when a placement falls through, and whether the recruiter you talk to actually knows your specialty.

Three concrete next steps:

1. **Verify membership directly** at NALTO's website rather than trusting the badge on an agency's homepage.
2. **Ask the agency specifically** how they handle credentialing — what their average turnaround is, who performs primary source verification, and what their process is for gaps in work history.
3. **Check for NALTO member agencies** in your specialty before going broader. The discipline matters more than the badge.
