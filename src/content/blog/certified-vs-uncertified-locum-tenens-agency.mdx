A hospital I knew once ran on locums for three months straight after their hospitalist group quit with two weeks' notice. The administrator told me later that the one question she wished she'd asked before signing any agency contract: "Are you NALTO-certified?" She hadn't. Two of the four physicians placed had credentialing gaps that nearly cost the facility its accreditation.

That story isn't unique. And yet most guides on picking a locum tenens agency treat certification like a footnote.

> **The Short Version:** For complex, high-stakes placements — hospitals, surgical specialties, multi-state coverage — agency certification (NALTO membership, NCQA-aligned processes) is a genuine safeguard, not a marketing badge. For shorter clinic stints, a non-member agency with a strong track record often does the job just fine. Know the difference before you sign.

**Key Takeaways**
- Credentialing can take 60–120 days at hospitals; certified agencies have the infrastructure to manage this without dropping the ball
- Credentialing applications can run 150 pages — agencies that pre-fill and liaise with facilities aren't a convenience, they're a necessity
- JCAHO-aligned malpractice pre-screening is standard at credible agencies; without it, providers carry far more risk
- Certification matters most when your placement involves hospital privileges, multiple states, or surgical procedures

## What "Certified" Actually Means (And What It Doesn't)

Here's what most people miss: there's no single government-issued "certified locum tenens agency" stamp. When the industry talks about certified agencies, they're usually referring to two things — **NALTO membership** (National Association of Locum Tenens Organizations) and **NCQA-aligned credentialing processes**. Some agencies also operate to JCAHO standards for malpractice and credential verification.

NALTO has a code of ethics. NCQA alignment means the agency follows a structured primary source verification process — meaning they don't take your word for your board certification, they call the board. That distinction matters enormously when a hospital's accreditation is on the line.

An uncertified agency isn't automatically shady. But they have no external accountability mechanism. If their credentialing team gets sloppy, nobody outside their office is checking.

## The Credentialing Stack: What Certified Agencies Actually Do

Before comparing the two types of agencies, you need to understand what full credentialing actually involves — because it's more than most people expect.

A complete credentialing file includes: CV, active state licenses, controlled substance registrations, DEA registration, medical school diplomas, post-graduate certifications, life support certs, immunization records, malpractice insurance certificate, procedure and case logs, and references. At least three references — two from clinicians in your specialty — covering competency over the past two years. If you finished training within the last nine months, your program director counts.

Hospital privileges verified in the process must have been held within the **last six months for at least six weeks**. Miss that window, and you're starting from scratch.

| Aspect | Certified / JCAHO-Aligned Agency | Uncertified Agency |
|---|---|---|
| Credential verification | Primary source checks — boards, licenses, DEA | Varies; potential gaps in rigor |
| Malpractice coverage | Agency-provided, pre-screened to JCAHO standards | Often provider-managed; higher burden on you |
| Application handling | Pre-fills 150-page applications; handles forms | Typically provider handles more independently |
| Multi-state licensing | Coordinated across states; handles DEA per state | May lack infrastructure for complex multi-state work |
| Facility liaison | Acts as privileging intermediary with facility | Ad hoc; less systematic |
| Hospital privilege timing | Tracks the 6-month/6-week verification windows | No guarantee |
| Timeline | Streamlined; 60–120 days typical | Independent FCVS route: longer, more expensive |

## When Certification Is Non-Negotiable

> **Reality Check:** If you're placing a surgeon into a hospital for a three-month contract across two states, cutting corners on agency credentialing is how hospitals end up on the news for the wrong reasons.

Certified agency standards become essential when:

**The facility is a hospital or health system.** Hospital credentialing follows bylaws that require primary source verification. An agency that pre-screens to JCAHO standards means the facility gets a file that already meets their internal requirements — speeding up the 60–120 day process rather than restarting it. AMN Healthcare describes this liaison role explicitly: agencies pre-fill applications, ensure regulatory compliance, and hand off a complete credential package so facilities don't have to chase down records.

**The placement spans multiple states.** Every state has its own licensing and DEA registration requirements. Certified agencies have the infrastructure — and often existing relationships with state medical boards — to manage this. Going independent without the Federation Credentials Verification Service (FCVS) is a logistics nightmare, and FCVS isn't cheap.

**The specialty is high-risk.** Anesthesia, surgery, emergency medicine — facilities are not going to privilege someone based on a PDF and a handshake. The malpractice pre-screening alone justifies the certified agency premium.

**Board certification is required.** Board-certified physicians access the widest range of locum opportunities. Certified agencies typically work with facilities that require it, and they verify it directly rather than assuming it.

## When Experience Outweighs Credentials

I'll be honest: not every locum placement needs the full NALTO-certified agency treatment.

Urgent care chains, federally qualified health centers, smaller clinics — many of these facilities approve locum credentialing in **as little as two weeks**. A smaller regional agency without formal NALTO membership but with 10 years of relationships in a specific state may place faster and negotiate better terms than a large certified shop that treats your assignment like ticket #4,471.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask any agency — certified or not — for their average credentialing timeline and what percentage of placements complete on that timeline. A certified agency that takes 180 days is worse than a nimble regional one that consistently delivers in 45.

For clinic and outpatient work, the relevant questions aren't really about certification. They're about: Does this agency know the facility? Have they placed there before? Do they have a recruiter who specializes in your specialty and region?

Niche depth matters. A general-purpose mega-agency may have NALTO membership and still struggle to place a rural psychiatrist because they lack the relationships. A smaller, focused agency without the badge sometimes wins on placement quality alone.

## The Independent Route: A Brief Warning

Some physicians consider going independent — no agency, just themselves and FCVS. FCVS functions as a credential repository that many states accept, reducing the burden of gathering documents repeatedly.

The problem: FCVS setup is not cheap, and it doesn't replace an agency's liaison function. You still negotiate contracts, manage housing and travel, handle malpractice coverage yourself, and chase down every facility-specific credentialing form. For a single short-term placement, that overhead rarely makes sense unless you already have a facility relationship.

For building a long-term locum practice across multiple facilities and states? Maybe. For most providers? The agency's administrative support — especially at a certified shop — is worth the fee.

---

## Practical Bottom Line

**If you're placing at a hospital, for a surgical specialty, or across multiple states:** only work with agencies that can articulate their credentialing process in detail, provide agency-covered malpractice pre-screened to JCAHO standards, and have a dedicated credentialing team — not a recruiter who also does credentialing on the side. NALTO membership is a reasonable proxy for this level of infrastructure.

**If you're placing for clinic or outpatient work:** evaluate agencies on track record, specialty focus, and facility relationships first. Certification is a plus, not a prerequisite.

**For any agency, certified or not, ask these three questions:**
1. What is your average credentialing-to-placement timeline for my specialty?
2. Who handles credentialing — a dedicated team or the recruiter?
3. Does the agency carry malpractice coverage, and to what standard?

The answers will tell you more than any badge.
