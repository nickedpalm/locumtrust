Finding a qualified locum tenens agency in [Jacksonville](/locations/florida/jacksonville/) shouldn't feel like a second job — but between the NALTO members, the credential mills, and the agencies that promise specialty depth they don't actually have, most administrators end up burning two weeks on calls before placing anyone. Jacksonville's market is unusually competitive: Baptist Health, Mayo Clinic Florida, and UF Health all draw from the same regional talent pool, which means your agency's bench and credentialing speed matter more here than in smaller metros.

## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Jacksonville

- **Verify NALTO membership and NCQA credentialing accreditation first.** These aren't vanity certifications — NALTO members follow a code of ethics around malpractice coverage handoffs, and NCQA credentialing means primary source verification is standardized, not ad hoc. Florida's licensing reciprocity is decent, but Jacksonville's level-one trauma centers won't onboard a locum without clean credentials regardless of urgency.
- **Ask specifically about Florida medical license expediting.** The Florida Board of Medicine isn't the fastest in the country. A top-tier agency will have established relationships with licensure specialists and a realistic timeline — typically 4–10 weeks for a new Florida license. If an agency quotes you "two weeks, no problem" without asking about current licensure status, walk away.
- **Probe their specialty bench for your gap, not their highlight reel.** An agency with 400 hospitalists on paper may have three available EM physicians in Northeast Florida at any given moment. Get specific: how many providers in your specialty are currently credentialed in Florida, available, and not already placed?
- **Understand the malpractice tail coverage structure before signing.** Most reputable agencies carry occurrence-based coverage, which is cleaner. Claims-made policies require a tail — confirm in writing who pays it and for how long. This comes up most often when a contract ends abruptly, which is exactly when you don't want ambiguity.
- **Check Joint Commission Certified Staffing Organization status if you're placing at a JC-accredited facility.** Baptist Medical Center Jacksonville and Mayo Clinic Florida both require this. Not every agency has it.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask for references from Jacksonville-area facilities specifically — not just "a hospital in Florida." An agency that regularly places in Duval County will know the credentialing coordinators, the parking logistics, and which facilities have housing stipend preferences. That institutional familiarity cuts days off a placement.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Jacksonville run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, duration, and urgency — with EM and surgical subspecialties commanding the top of that range, especially for short-notice weekend coverage. The agency typically marks up 20–35% over the physician's daily rate, and that spread covers credentialing, licensing, malpractice, travel, and housing. Figure 2–6 weeks from initial contact to first shift for a credentialed provider; first-time Florida licensure adds time.

> **Reality Check:** Administrators consistently underestimate the total cost by ignoring the agency fee on top of the physician rate. If an agency quotes you a "provider cost" without being explicit about what's included, ask them to break out their fee separately. The lowest daily rate with a 35% markup often costs more than a slightly higher rate with a 22% markup.

## Local Market Overview

Jacksonville is the largest city by land area in the contiguous United States, which creates genuine coverage geography challenges — a locum placed at UF Health North is not the same commute as one placed at St. Vincent's Riverside, and agencies that don't flag this upfront create retention problems mid-contract. The city's status as a major Navy logistics hub also means a higher-than-average share of military-affiliated patients at Naval Hospital Jacksonville, creating periodic staffing pressure that ripples into the surrounding civilian health system.
