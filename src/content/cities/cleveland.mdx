## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Cleveland

- **Verify NALTO membership and Joint Commission certification first.** These aren't vanity badges — NALTO members are bound by a code of ethics on fee transparency and candidate representation. Joint Commission-certified staffing organizations have passed external audits on credentialing processes. Start here before you read a single case study.
- **Ask about Ohio licensing speed specifically.** Ohio's Medical Board processes locum applications on its own timeline, and agencies vary wildly in how proactively they manage state licensing. Ask for an average days-to-license figure for Ohio-placed physicians in the last 12 months. If they can't answer that, keep moving.
- **Match specialty depth to your gap.** A firm with strong hospitalist and EM coverage may have a thin bench in subspecialties like interventional cardiology or pediatric neurology. Cleveland's tertiary care environment means you may need niche coverage — confirm the agency has placed in your specialty in Ohio, not just "the Midwest."
- **Understand the guarantee policy before you sign.** Top-tier agencies guarantee replacement at no additional cost if a placed provider leaves within the first 30 days. Get this in writing. Some firms bury guarantee carve-outs in the contract for cancellations under 72 hours.
- **Check malpractice coverage structure.** Confirm occurrence-based vs. claims-made, and verify tail coverage responsibility. For multi-month contracts in high-acuity settings, this is not a detail — it's a liability exposure question.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask your contact at any agency if they have a dedicated credentialing team or if one person handles both recruitment and credentialing. At high-volume shops, those functions are separated for a reason — combined roles are a flag for slower turnaround when you can't afford it.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Cleveland typically run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, contract length, and whether the agency handles travel and housing logistics or passes those costs through. Emergency medicine and surgical subspecialties sit at the upper end; primary care and general hospitalist coverage trends lower. Most agencies quote an all-in rate that bundles the physician fee, malpractice, and benefits — but the spread between agencies on that all-in number can be 15–25% for the same specialty.

> **Reality Check:** The most common mistake administrators make is comparing base rates without accounting for what's included. One agency quoting $180/hour and covering malpractice tail is often a better deal than a competitor at $160/hour where tail coverage is your problem. Always normalize to total cost of placement, not the hourly rate.

Standard credentialing timelines run 2–4 weeks for a licensed physician already active in Ohio, longer for out-of-state providers needing a new state license. If you need someone in 10 days, lead with that constraint and ask agencies to tell you honestly whether it's achievable — the ones who say "no problem" without asking follow-up questions are the ones who will miss the deadline.

## Local Market Overview

Cleveland operates in a high-competition healthcare labor environment — the Clinic, UH, MetroHealth, and a dense ring of regional systems all pull from the same specialist pool, which means qualified physicians have options and locum rates here reflect that pressure. Ohio's certificate-of-need history and strong hospital system consolidation also mean administrators are often backfilling highly specialized roles, not generalist ones — specialty-depth in your agency relationship matters more here than in less consolidated markets.
