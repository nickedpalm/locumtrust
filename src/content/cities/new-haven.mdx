Finding a qualified locum tenens agency in [New Haven](/locations/connecticut/new-haven/) shouldn't take longer than filling the shift itself — but between the agencies that overpromise on credentialing speed and the ones that quietly lack coverage in your specialty, the vetting process eats time most hospital administrators don't have. New Haven's healthcare market is unusually dense for a city its size: Yale New Haven Health dominates, but the independent practices, FQHCs, and community hospitals competing in its shadow all face the same staffing crunch, and the same agencies are pitching all of them simultaneously.

> **The Short Version:** For most New Haven facilities, the fastest path to a vetted locum is a NALTO-member agency with demonstrated Connecticut licensing experience and a malpractice tail already structured. Credentialing speed is the real differentiator — not the size of the agency's roster. Below, I break down exactly what to look for.

## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in New Haven

- **Verify NALTO membership before anything else.** NALTO members are contractually bound to ethical placement standards and primary source verification. Non-members aren't. This one filter eliminates most of the agencies you don't want.
- **Ask specifically about Connecticut licensing timelines.** CT has its own medical licensing quirks — some agencies have a streamlined pipeline into the state, others don't. Get a specific number of days, not "we handle it."
- **Confirm malpractice tail coverage in writing.** Most reputable agencies carry occurrence-based coverage, but some issue claims-made policies that leave a gap when the engagement ends. Get the actual policy language before signing.
- **Request specialty-specific placement history in the region.** An agency that's placed 50 hospitalists nationally but never worked a New England academic environment is a different product than one that's staffed Yale affiliate sites before.
- **Check for Joint Commission Certified Staffing Organization or NCQA Credentialing Accreditation.** These aren't just logos — they represent independently audited credentialing processes. For a facility with its own accreditation at stake, this matters.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask the agency how many locums they currently have active in Connecticut. An agency with five active placements in-state has built relationships with the licensing board and knows the local quirks. An agency starting from scratch on your timeline is a risk.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in this market run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, duration, and urgency — emergency medicine and surgical subspecialties sit at the high end, primary care and family medicine toward the lower. Most agencies work on a markup model over the physician's hourly rate, typically 30–50%, so the total engagement cost includes travel, housing, and malpractice on top of compensation. Credentialing-to-start timelines for a straightforward placement run two to six weeks; complex privileging at a Joint Commission-accredited facility can push eight.

> **Reality Check:** The single most common mistake is treating all-in cost as the only metric. A $10,000 cheaper placement that takes three extra weeks to credential costs more in operational disruption than the savings — especially when you're already running one provider short in a busy department.

## Local Market Overview

New Haven's healthcare ecosystem anchors around Yale New Haven Health and its affiliates, but the city also hosts a significant concentration of federally qualified health centers serving one of Connecticut's higher-density urban populations — which means demand for primary care and behavioral health locums tracks above state averages. The presence of Yale School of Medicine creates a sophisticated buyer: administrators here tend to know the questions to ask, which is exactly why the agencies cutting corners on credentialing don't last long in this market.
