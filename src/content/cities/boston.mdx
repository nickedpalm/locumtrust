## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Boston

- **Verify NALTO membership first, then ask about Massachusetts licensing timelines.** MA has one of the slower state medical board turnaround times in the country — a credentialed agency that doesn't flag this upfront will burn your timeline. Ask specifically: "What's your average days-to-placement for Massachusetts licenses?" Anything over 45 days without a parallel-processing explanation is a red flag.
- **Check specialty depth, not breadth.** A firm claiming to cover 50 specialties with a 12-person placement team is lying to you with math. If you're covering a neurology or orthopedic surgery gap at a Boston teaching hospital, you need an agency that lives in that subspecialty — one with existing credentialed candidates, not a recruiter who'll start cold-calling tomorrow.
- **Ask for their malpractice tail coverage policy in writing.** Massachusetts is a high-litigation state. Tail coverage disputes are how you end up with a staffing gap *and* a legal problem. NALTO members are contractually required to provide tail coverage, but the specifics of limits and retroactive dates vary. Get it in the contract before your CMO signs anything.
- **Demand a credentialing timeline guarantee, not just a process description.** Joint Commission Certified agencies and those with NCQA Credentialing Accreditation carry an independent audit trail you can reference. An agency's internal "rigorous process" means nothing without third-party verification — and in a market like Boston, where your medical staff office is already stretched thin, you need to trust the primary source verification is actually happening.
- **Reference-check against New England health systems specifically.** An agency that's placed 200 locums in rural Texas may have zero relationships with Partners Healthcare, Beth Israel Deaconess, or Tufts Medical Center. Boston's hospital network has its own credentialing preferences and privilege structures. Regional experience isn't a nice-to-have — it's months off your timeline.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask the agency rep: "Who was your last placement at a Boston-area academic medical center, and can I call that medical staff office?" If they hedge, they're not your vendor.

## What to Expect

Contract values for locum tenens placements in Boston run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, duration, and whether travel and housing are bundled — with surgical subspecialties and psychiatry consistently at the upper end given Massachusetts's chronic shortage in both. Most agencies work on a markup model over the locum's daily rate, and standard engagement timelines from initial contact to first shift run 3–6 weeks for credentialed candidates, longer if Massachusetts licensing is required from scratch.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake administrators make is comparing agency quotes on day rate alone without accounting for benefits burden, malpractice coverage, and housing. An agency quoting $50/hour less may be passing those costs back to you in a different line item — or cutting corners on tail coverage that surfaces 18 months later as your problem, not theirs. Build a full cost comparison before you negotiate.

## Local Market Overview

Boston sits in a dense academic medical corridor — Harvard, Tufts, and Boston University all feed teaching hospital networks that run on complex credentialing timelines and have little tolerance for agencies that don't understand the difference between provisional privileges and full staff appointment. The city's concentration of Magnet-designated hospitals also means your locum placement will face more rigorous peer review than in most markets, which rewards agencies with thorough primary source verification over those who paper over gaps.
