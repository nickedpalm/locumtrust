## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Fargo

- **Verify NALTO membership before anything else.** NALTO members are bound to a code of ethics that covers transparent fee disclosure and honest candidate representation. Non-members have no accountability structure — and in a tight market like North Dakota's, cut-rate agencies have real incentive to oversell candidate availability.
- **Ask about North Dakota licensing timelines specifically.** ND medical board processing runs 6–12 weeks for full licensure. Top agencies have in-house licensing teams that know the board's idiosyncrasies and can run concurrent verification while you're still in contract negotiations. If an agency is vague about timelines, that's your answer.
- **Confirm malpractice coverage structure upfront.** You want occurrence-based coverage, not claims-made. Claims-made policies leave tail exposure that can land back on your facility. Ask for the certificate before the provider sets foot in your building.
- **Probe their rural and critical access hospital experience.** Fargo is the regional hub, but many engagements here involve facilities serving western ND and eastern MT. An agency placing hospitalists and ER physicians in CAH environments needs a fundamentally different bench than one that only works urban systems.
- **Check their credentialing speed guarantee.** Best-in-class agencies credential in 30 days or less. If they can't give you a number, they don't have a process.

> **Pro Tip:** Ask your agency rep how many locum physicians they've placed at Sanford or Essentia specifically. Agencies that have done business with your system have pre-established credentialing pathways — that can shave two to three weeks off your onboarding timeline.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Fargo typically run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, duration, and call burden — hospitalists and urgent care MDs sit toward the lower end, while surgical subspecialties and proceduralists push toward the ceiling. The agency handles sourcing, credentialing, licensing, travel, and malpractice; you pay a markup over the provider's base rate that typically runs 25–40%. Most agencies can get a candidate in front of your credentialing committee within two to four weeks if you're working with an organized shop.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake administrators make is comparing agency markups without accounting for total cost-of-vacancy. A $12,000/week locum ER physician sounds expensive until you price out diverted ambulances, strained staff overtime, and the reputational hit of a closed service line. The agency fee is rarely the most expensive thing in the equation.
