## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Oklahoma City

- **Verify NALTO membership and NCQA credentialing accreditation before any other conversation.** These aren't rubber stamps — NALTO members are bound to a code of ethics that prohibits the bait-and-switch CVs that plague unaffiliated brokers. NCQA credentialing accreditation means primary source verification is actually happening, not just being invoiced.
- **Ask specifically about Oklahoma medical licensing turnaround.** Oklahoma requires a completed application plus board verification from every state the provider has ever held a license. Agencies that have placed frequently in Oklahoma know this can add 3–6 weeks to timelines; the ones that don't will quote you two weeks and disappear.
- **Confirm malpractice tail coverage is occurrence-based, not claims-made.** Claims-made policies leave your facility exposed after the engagement ends. Any credible agency includes occurrence-based coverage as a default — if they're pitching claims-made as standard, walk.
- **Get a specialty depth audit, not just a headcount.** A national agency with 40,000 providers in their database sounds impressive until you need a nocturnist for OU Health or a rural outreach hospitalist for Integris Canadian Valley. Ask how many of their placed providers have Oklahoma ties in the last 18 months — specific specialty, specific geography.
- **Nail down the guarantee policy before signing.** Top-tier agencies offer a replacement guarantee if a placement doesn't work out within the first week or two. Anything less than a written replacement commitment is a red flag.

> **Pro Tip:** Oklahoma City's two major health systems — OU Health and Integris Health — both have credentialing offices with specific documentation requirements that differ from the state board minimums. Ask your agency whether they have a credentialing coordinator who has worked directly with these systems. The answer will tell you everything about how many placements they've actually made here.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Oklahoma City typically run $20,000–$150,000 per engagement depending on specialty, duration, and urgency — hospitalists and primary care fill at the lower end, proceduralists and subspecialists at the top. Most agencies work on a contingency model (no placement, no fee), with the agency markup baked into the provider's bill rate rather than charged separately to the facility. Expect 2–4 weeks for a standard placement from intake call to first shift, longer for specialties requiring DEA registration or hospital privileging.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake isn't overpaying the bill rate — it's underestimating the administrative cost of a bad placement. If a locum no-shows or fails credentialing on day three, you're back to square one plus the time cost of two credentialing cycles. Agencies with Joint Commission Certified Staffing Organization status carry independent auditing of their quality processes; the markup difference versus a non-certified shop is almost always worth it.
