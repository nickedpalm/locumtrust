Finding a locum tenens agency in [Washington](/locations/district-of-columbia/washington-dc/), DC shouldn't feel like navigating a federal procurement process — but for most CMOs and administrators at the District's major health systems, that's exactly what it becomes. Between MedStar, Children's National, Howard University Hospital, and the VA Medical Center all competing for the same shrinking pool of temporary physicians, the agencies that actually deliver here are a short list. This directory cuts through the noise so you can find them without wasting three weeks on calls with staffing reps who've never placed a provider east of the Mississippi.

## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Washington

- **Verify NALTO membership before anything else.** Non-member agencies aren't bound by the code of ethics that governs fee transparency, candidate exclusivity, and non-compete language. In a city where hospital legal teams scrutinize every contract, this matters.
- **Ask specifically about DC licensure timelines.** DC Medical Board processing runs 8–14 weeks for full licensure — agencies that don't front-load this will blow your coverage window. Ask for their average credentialing-to-start time for DC-based placements, not national averages.
- **Confirm Joint Commission or NCQA credentialing accreditation.** Federal facilities (VA, DoD, federally qualified health centers) and many DC health systems require primary source verification that meets these standards. An agency without it can't place into a meaningful chunk of the DC market.
- **Probe their specialty depth in your specific area.** Generalist shops can find you an internist. Specialty-focused agencies have the bench depth for hospitalist coverage at 2 AM on a federal holiday — which in DC, there are more of than you'd think.
- **Check their malpractice tail coverage policy in writing.** Some agencies offer occurrence-based coverage; others offer claims-made with tail — and the difference becomes your problem if a claim surfaces after the engagement ends.

> **Pro Tip:** DC's hybrid federal-civilian patient mix is unusual. Ask your agency shortlist how many placements they've made specifically into federally-affiliated facilities in the last 12 months. The ones who hedge or go vague haven't done it recently.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Washington typically run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, engagement length, and whether travel and housing are bundled — primary care hospitalist coverage on the lower end, surgical subspecialties and anesthesiology at the top. Most reputable agencies can move from initial contact to a credentialed candidate presentation in 3–7 business days for common specialties, though DC licensure requirements will push your actual start date out further.

> **Reality Check:** The biggest pricing mistake administrators make is comparing agency bill rates without accounting for what's included. Agency A at $180/hour with malpractice, housing, and travel covered is a different number than Agency B at $160/hour where you're sourcing your own housing near Capitol Hill — which, as a reminder, is not cheap.

## Local Market Overview

Washington's healthcare landscape is shaped by its unusual density of federal health facilities, academic medical centers, and underserved community health centers operating under the same geographic footprint — all of which creates perpetual staffing pressure that makes locum coverage not an emergency measure but a standard operating tool. The District's mandatory healthcare compact with surrounding Maryland and Virginia providers also means multi-state licensure flexibility is a genuine differentiator when evaluating agencies.
