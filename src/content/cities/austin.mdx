Finding a reliable locum tenens agency in [Austin](/locations/texas/austin/) shouldn't feel like running a parallel hiring process on top of the one you're already behind on — but here we are. The Austin market is tighter than administrators expect: UT Dell Medical School's rapid expansion and a wave of new urgent care chains have pulled available locums inventory in multiple directions, and agencies that are great at placing family medicine in rural Texas don't always have the specialty bench depth you need for a downtown academic medical center.

## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Austin

- **Verify NALTO membership and Joint Commission certification first.** These aren't vanity credentials — NALTO members agree to a code of ethics that prohibits bait-and-switch billing, and Joint Commission-certified staffing organizations undergo independent audits of their credentialing processes. Ask for documentation, not just a claim on their website.
- **Ask about Texas-specific licensing speed.** Texas Medical Board processing times vary wildly by specialty. A strong agency will know current TMB wait times, have a dedicated licensing team for Texas, and tell you upfront whether they can get a candidate privileged at your facility before your gap hits. If they hedge, that's your answer.
- **Demand NCQA credentialing accreditation or an equivalent primary source verification audit trail.** Austin's major health systems — including Ascension Seton and St. David's — have credentialing standards that will bounce an agency's candidate if documentation gaps exist. Your agency should be sending you a complete credentialing packet, not asking your MSO to do their homework.
- **Pressure-test their Austin bench.** Ask how many locums they currently have placed within 150 miles of Austin and how many have active Texas licenses. A national agency with a thin regional network will quote you a candidate from [Phoenix](/locations/arizona/phoenix/) who's never worked Texas hospital systems and expects you to be grateful.
- **Clarify the guarantee policy before you sign.** If a placed physician leaves mid-contract for any reason, what happens? Top-tier agencies offer a free replacement or prorated refund. Some don't. Read the contract.

> **Pro Tip:** Run your shortlist through the NALTO member directory at nalto.org and cross-check against the Joint Commission's Certified Organizations database. Takes ten minutes and immediately eliminates agencies inflating their credentials.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Austin typically run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, contract length, and whether you're covering nights and weekends — hospitalist and emergency medicine placements skew toward the top end. Agencies layer in malpractice coverage, travel, and housing on top of physician compensation, so your all-in cost will run 25–40% above the stated daily rate. Credentialing-to-first-shift timelines range from two weeks (for a candidate already licensed in Texas with active hospital privileges nearby) to eight weeks for a new-to-Texas physician who needs full TMB licensure.

> **Reality Check:** The most common budgeting mistake is comparing a locum's "bill rate" to a permanent physician's base salary. You're paying for speed, flexibility, and zero benefits burden. The real comparison is locum cost vs. the revenue leakage from an unfilled shift — and at Austin's patient volumes, that math usually tilts hard toward filling the gap.

## Local Market Overview

Austin's healthcare market is genuinely unusual: a metro growing faster than its hospital infrastructure, anchored by Dell Seton Medical Center at The University of Texas and a dense ring of suburban health systems all competing for the same specialist pool. That growth has made locum tenens less of a stopgap and more of an ongoing staffing strategy for Austin administrators — agencies that understand the local credentialing landscape and have warm relationships with Austin-area facilities will get your candidate through privileges faster than a generalist national firm running your case from a call center.
