Finding a qualified locum tenens agency in [Miami](/locations/florida/miami/) shouldn't feel like a second job — but between the volume of national firms that don't understand South Florida's multilingual patient base and the agencies that promise 48-hour placement then disappear into credentialing purgatory, most CMOs end up doing more vetting than they bargained for. This directory cuts through it. Every agency listed here has been reviewed against NALTO membership, credentialing standards, and specialty depth so you're not starting from scratch.

## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Miami

- **Verify NALTO membership and NCQA or Joint Commission credentialing accreditation first.** These aren't vanity badges — they mean the agency has standardized primary source verification, which is the difference between a provider who's ready day one and one who gets flagged at credentialing. Florida's licensing board (FLBOM) moves slower than most; a credentialed agency knows how to route around the delays.
- **Ask specifically about Spanish-speaking and Haitian Creole-speaking provider availability.** Miami-Dade has one of the highest proportions of non-English-speaking patients in the country. An agency that can't address this isn't a fit for most facilities here — and they probably don't have the regional bench depth to place well anyway.
- **Confirm their malpractice coverage structure before signing anything.** Occurrence-based coverage is standard for locums; claims-made coverage with no tail is a liability you'll inherit. Get it in writing.
- **Ask for average time-to-placement by specialty, not overall.** A firm that's fast in primary care but slow in, say, hospitalist or emergency medicine will sell you the aggregate number. Miami Health District and [Jackson](/locations/mississippi/jackson/) Health System have tight turnaround expectations — specialty-specific placement timelines matter.
- **Check their guarantee policy.** Top-tier agencies offer a replacement guarantee if a provider leaves within the first 30 days. If an agency won't commit to this, that tells you something about their confidence in their own screening.

> **Pro Tip:** Florida requires locum physicians to hold an active Florida medical license — there's no reciprocity shortcut. The best agencies pre-stage licensing applications before you even sign the contract. Ask whether they start the Florida licensure process at engagement or at placement. The answer tells you everything about their operational discipline.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Miami typically run $20,000–$150,000 per engagement depending on specialty, duration, and urgency — emergency medicine and surgical subspecialties command the top of that range, while primary care and internal medicine sit in the middle third. The agency fee is typically baked into the bill rate, not charged separately, so you're negotiating the all-in hourly or daily rate rather than a placement fee on top. Most credentialing-ready placements take two to four weeks; emergency placements can happen faster, but expect to pay a premium.

> **Reality Check:** The most common pricing mistake is comparing bill rates across agencies without confirming what's included. Malpractice tail coverage, travel, housing, and licensing fees are sometimes excluded from the headline rate. Get a full cost-of-placement breakdown in writing — agencies that won't provide one aren't worth your time.

## Local Market Overview

Miami's healthcare market is dense and competitive — Jackson Health System, Baptist Health, and a large network of federally qualified health centers create constant, overlapping demand for locum coverage, especially in specialties serving underinsured and uninsured populations. The city's high physician turnover rate (driven partly by cost of living and partly by the volume of locums-to-perm pipelines that fall through) means agencies with genuine Florida provider networks — not just national rosters with a few Miami names appended — are worth the premium. If an agency can't name specific facilities they've placed in across Miami-Dade County, they probably haven't.
