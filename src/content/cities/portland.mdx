Finding a qualified locum tenens agency in [Portland](/locations/oregon/portland/) shouldn't require a PhD in vendor evaluation — but between the agencies that overpromise on credentialing speed and the ones that disappear after placement, most hospital administrators get burned at least once before they find a reliable partner. Portland's health system is dense and competitive: OHSU, [Providence](/locations/rhode-island/providence/), Legacy, and PeaceHealth all operate here, which means the agency pool is large but quality varies wildly. This directory cuts through it.

## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Portland

- **Verify NALTO membership before anything else.** NALTO members are bound by a code of ethics that governs contract transparency, fee disclosure, and physician vetting. In a market where Oregon's licensing board is notoriously slow (average 8–12 weeks for full licensure), working with an agency that has pre-credentialed Oregon-licensed physicians on the bench is the difference between a covered shift and a staffing crisis.
- **Ask specifically about Oregon malpractice tail coverage.** Oregon requires continuous coverage for claims-made policies. Some agencies hand this liability back to the facility in the fine print. Get written confirmation that tail coverage is included for the duration and beyond.
- **Check specialty depth, not just headcount.** An agency that places 10,000 providers nationally but has three hospitalists in the Pacific Northwest isn't useful when your ER needs coverage Monday. Ask for a bench count specific to Oregon and neighboring states (WA, ID) for your specialty.
- **Demand a credentialing timeline guarantee.** Best-in-class agencies with NCQA Credentialing Accreditation or a Joint Commission Certified Staffing designation can complete primary source verification in 3–5 business days. If an agency can't commit to a window in writing, that's the answer.
- **Reference-check on guarantee policies.** If a placed provider leaves early or underperforms, what happens? Top agencies offer free replacement within 30–60 days. Get this in the contract, not just in the sales call.

> **Pro Tip:** Oregon participates in the Interstate Medical Licensure Compact (IMLC), which can dramatically speed up licensure for physicians already licensed in compact states. Ask any agency you're evaluating whether their credentialing team actively uses IMLC pathways — the ones who don't are leaving weeks on the table.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in Portland typically run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, duration, and urgency — an ER physician covering a holiday weekend sits at a very different price point than a subspecialist filling a four-month gap. Most agencies charge a markup of 20–35% over the physician's daily rate, bundled into a single bill rate that includes housing, travel, and malpractice. Expect 2–4 weeks from signed contract to first shift for standard engagements, or 72 hours for emergency placements from agencies with active Oregon benches.

> **Reality Check:** Facilities often compare agency bill rates without accounting for what's included. An agency quoting $180/hour with housing and tail coverage included can be cheaper than one quoting $155/hour where you're sourcing housing separately and eating the malpractice tail. Build a total-cost comparison before signing anything.

## Local Market Overview

Portland's healthcare market is simultaneously one of the most progressive and one of the most operationally complex in the Pacific Northwest — OHSU's academic medical center creates a high bar for credentialing standards, and the region's rural catchment area (stretching into eastern Oregon and southern [Washington](/locations/district-of-columbia/washington-dc/)) means demand for specialty coverage routinely outpaces local supply. Agencies with established relationships in this corridor, particularly those covering critical access hospitals along I-84 and US-97, are worth paying a modest premium for.
