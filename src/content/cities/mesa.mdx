## How to Choose a Locum Tenens Agency in Mesa

- **Verify NALTO membership first.** NALTO members agree to a code of ethics around transparency on fees, candidate representation, and contract terms. An agency that isn't a member isn't automatically bad — but it's a yellow flag worth probing.
- **Ask specifically about Arizona licensure speed.** The Arizona Medical Board processes expedited licenses, but your agency needs to know the system. Ask how many Arizona placements they've completed in the last 12 months and what their average credentialing-to-start timeline looks like. Under 30 days is achievable for most specialties.
- **Match specialty depth to your gap.** A generalist agency will find you a hospitalist. If you need a pediatric hospitalist or a nocturnist comfortable with high-acuity desert trauma volume, you want an agency with a deep bench in that specialty, not one that's going to blast a job post and hope.
- **Get the malpractice terms in writing.** Tail coverage questions kill more locum arrangements than anything else. Confirm whether the agency provides occurrence-based or claims-made coverage, and who carries the tail if the contract ends.
- **Check the guarantee policy.** Top-tier agencies will back-fill at no additional cost if a placed provider leaves mid-contract. Ask for it explicitly — not every agency volunteers this.

> **Pro Tip:** Mesa's Banner Health and Dignity Health systems both operate major facilities in the East Valley. If you're placing into either system, ask your agency whether they have existing credentialing relationships there — it can cut two to three weeks off the startup timeline.

## What to Expect

Locum tenens contracts in the Phoenix metro typically run $20,000–$150,000 depending on specialty, duration, and whether the role includes call coverage. Primary care and hospitalist engagements tend to land in the lower-to-mid range; procedural specialties, anesthesia, and psychiatry push toward the top. Expect to pay an agency markup of 25–40% above the provider's hourly rate — that's covering credentialing, licensing, malpractice, housing, and travel logistics, so it's not pure margin.

> **Reality Check:** The most common mistake administrators make is comparing the agency's all-in bill rate to what they'd pay a permanent employee's base salary. That's an apples-to-oranges comparison. The real comparison is agency bill rate versus the total cost of a vacant position — lost revenue, diverted patients, overtime for existing staff — which in a busy Mesa primary care clinic can run $15,000–$40,000 per month.

Agency onboarding for a new client typically takes 48–72 hours for paperwork; candidate presentation follows within a few days for common specialties, longer for subspecialties. Build in three to four weeks from first call to provider start date if you need Arizona licensure; less if the candidate is already licensed in-state.
